Abstract

OBJECTIVES: We present a novel technique to provide full diastolic opening even in advanced disease. Rheumatic mitral stenosis is predominantly a disease of commissural fusion in addition to leaflet and sub-valvular thickening and fusion. Commissural fusion results in contraction of the secondary orifice of mitral valve formed by the free margins of the leaflets. Sub-valvular fusion and thickening results in contraction and restriction of tertiary orifice of mitral valve. Standard Carpentier’s techniques address restriction at both these levels by commissurotomy to enlarge secondary orifice and other sub-valvular procedures for the tertiary orifice. Traditionally, commissurotomy is extended laterally upto 5 mm. of the annulus on both sides. On coming-off bypass, majority of these cases still have high gradients (5-8mmHg or more), anterior leaflet doming, incomplete separation of both leaflets and a turbulent, mosaic pattern of flow through the mitral valve. It is difficult to get a valve area of >1.75 Cm2 most of the times. In short, most of these valves still have residual stenosis even after utilising all the classical Carpentier’s techniques (Fig.1A). We developed this technique to completely abolish commissural fusion and achieve full diastolic opening of the valve, giving valve areas close to 3 Cm2, no anterior leaflet doming and a laminar flow through the mitral orifice.

METHODS: On both sides, the commissurotomy is extended up-to the annulus and then along the annulus up-to the trigones (Fig.1C). Anterior leaflet is then pushed into the ventricle to achieve the fully opened position. A triangle is made with the trigone as apex, cut margin of anterior leaflet on one side and annulus and part of posterior leaflet on the other side. This triangular defect is measured and filled by an appropriately sized triangular gluteraldehyde tanned pericardial patch or more recently, tissue engineered collagen matrix patch using 5-0 prolene. Only the sides of the triangle are attached ; anterior leaflet to one side and annulus to the other. Base of the patch remains free and to prevent it’s prolapse, it is attached , in it’s middle, to the commissural head of papillary muscles by a PTFE CV-6 suture (Fig.1D).

RESULTS: Extended Commissurotomy has been used in 12 patients of severe rheumatic mitral stenosis from July 2016 to December 2016. Post-operatively, mean pressure gradients were 3.14±0.86 mmHg and mean valve area 2.95±0.23Cm2 (pressure-half time method). There were no commissural leaks, no doming of leaflets and a laminar flow (Fig.1B).

CONCLUSIONS: Since long, rheumatic repair surgeons have struggled with residual stenosis in form of doming, high gradients, small valve areas. By directly enlarging the secondary orifice of mitral valve , present method provides the solution in a scientific manner. It has the potential to further increase the reparability rates of rheumatic mitral valves beyond the current 70%.
